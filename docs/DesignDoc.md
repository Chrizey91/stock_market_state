***Stock Market Indicators***
1. Sentiment Indicators

These measure how optimistic or fearful investors are.

Fear & Greed Index
Combines volatility, momentum, options activity, safe-haven demand, etc.
Extreme fear often signals good long-term buying opportunities.
Extreme greed often appears near market tops.
AAII Sentiment Survey
Bullish vs bearish retail investors.
Put/Call Ratio
High ratio = fear/hedging.
Low ratio = optimism/speculation.
Consumer Confidence Surveys
Useful because consumers drive much of economic activity.
2. Smart Money vs Dumb Money

These attempt to distinguish institutional investors from retail investors.

Smart Money Confidence Index
Dumb Money Confidence Index
Insider Buying/Selling
Company executives often know their businesses best.
Heavy insider buying is usually a positive signal.
Hedge Fund Net Exposure
How aggressively hedge funds are positioned.
Institutional Fund Flows
Money moving into or out of equities.
3. Federal Reserve & Central Banks

Arguably the most important macro driver.

Federal Reserve interest rate decisions
Fed Funds Rate
Quantitative Tightening (QT)
Quantitative Easing (QE)
Dot Plot projections
Real Interest Rates
Liquidity measures

Key principle:

Markets generally like falling rates and abundant liquidity.

4. Volatility & Fear
VIX
Often called the "fear gauge."

Typical interpretation:

VIX	Meaning
<15	Complacency
15-25	Normal
25-35	Elevated fear
>35	Panic
5. Market Breadth

These tell you whether the market rally is broad or driven by a few stocks.

Advance/Decline Line
Percentage of stocks above 200-day moving average
Percentage of stocks above 50-day moving average
New Highs vs New Lows

A healthy bull market usually has broad participation.

6. Credit Market Indicators

Many professionals watch credit markets more closely than stocks.

High Yield Spread

Difference between junk bonds and Treasury yields.

Narrow spread = confidence
Wide spread = fear
Corporate Bond Spreads
Investment Grade spreads
High Yield spreads
Credit Default Swaps (CDS)

Often signal stress before stocks react.

7. Economic Indicators

These tell you whether the economy is accelerating or slowing.

Leading Indicators
ISM Manufacturing PMI
ISM Services PMI
New Orders Index
Building Permits
Initial Jobless Claims
Growth Indicators
GDP Growth
Unemployment Rate
Wage Growth
Retail Sales
Industrial Production
8. Liquidity Indicators

Many investors underestimate liquidity.

Watch:

M2 Money Supply
Reverse Repo Facility usage
Treasury General Account balance
Central Bank balance sheets
Bank lending growth

Liquidity often explains market moves that fundamentals alone cannot.

A Simple "Market Dashboard"

If I could only track 10 indicators, I'd choose:

Fear & Greed Index
VIX
Fed Funds Rate
10Y Treasury Yield
2Y vs 10Y Yield Curve
ISM Manufacturing PMI
High Yield Credit Spread
% of S&P 500 stocks above 200-day MA
Insider Buying/Selling Ratio
M2 Liquidity Growth

Together these give a surprisingly complete picture of:

Investor sentiment
Risk appetite
Economic growth
Credit stress
Liquidity conditions
Market internals

For someone with your long-term ETF-oriented approach, I would pay the most attention to liquidity, credit spreads, market breadth, and valuation, and the least attention to daily news and short-term sentiment swings. Those four areas tend to have the strongest connection to major bull and bear markets.
