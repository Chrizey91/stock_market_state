If your goal is to answer "Is the S&P 500 healthy?", I'd separate the indicators into four layers:

Price health (What is the market doing?)
Participation (How many stocks are contributing?)
Fundamental health (Are companies actually performing well?)
Valuation (Is the market reasonably priced?)

Here's the checklist I would use.

1. Trend & Momentum (Highest Priority)

These answer: Is the market in an uptrend?

S&P 500 above the 200-day Moving Average
Above = healthy long-term trend
Below = caution
50-day vs 200-day MA
Golden Cross (50 > 200) = bullish
Death Cross = bearish
Higher highs / higher lows

Simple but powerful.

Healthy markets continue making new highs over time.

2. Market Breadth (Very Important)

One of the best predictors of market strength.

% of stocks above 200-day MA

Healthy bull markets:

70–90%

Weak markets:

below 50%

Danger zone:

below 30%

Advance-Decline Line

Ask:

Are many stocks rising?

or

Are only a few mega caps carrying the market?

A rising S&P with a falling A/D Line is a warning.

New Highs vs New Lows

Healthy market:

many new highs
few new lows
Equal Weight S&P 500 vs Cap Weight

Compare:

Standard S&P500
Equal Weight S&P500

If only the largest companies are rising, the equal-weight index will lag significantly.

That indicates narrowing participation.

3. Earnings (Arguably Most Important)

Ultimately, stocks follow earnings.

EPS Growth

Growing earnings = healthy.

Negative earnings growth usually pressures stocks.

Revenue Growth

Revenue is harder to manipulate than earnings.

Look for:

accelerating sales
broad growth
Earnings Revisions

Analysts constantly revise earnings.

Upward revisions:

✓ bullish

Downward revisions:

✗ bearish

Profit Margins

Are companies becoming more profitable?

Expanding margins are an excellent sign.

4. Valuation

Healthy doesn't necessarily mean cheap.

Forward P/E

Compare current P/E against historical average.

Example:

15 = historically inexpensive

22 = expensive

30 = extremely expensive

CAPE Ratio (Shiller PE)

Useful for long-term valuation.

Not useful for short-term timing.

Earnings Yield

Inverse of P/E.

Compare with Treasury yields.

5. Economic Backdrop

The S&P usually performs best when the economy expands.

Watch:

GDP growth
ISM PMI (>50)
Employment
Consumer spending
6. Credit Markets

Stocks dislike credit stress.

Monitor:

High Yield spreads
Investment Grade spreads

If spreads suddenly widen:

⚠ Risk is increasing.

7. Volatility
VIX

Healthy bull markets:

10–20

Stress:

20–30

Panic:

35+

8. Liquidity

Markets love liquidity.

Track:

Fed balance sheet
M2 money supply
Bank lending
Treasury liquidity
9. Leadership

Ask:

Who is leading?

Healthy bull markets usually have leadership from multiple sectors.

Examples:

Technology
Industrials
Financials
Consumer Discretionary
Healthcare

Weak markets often rely on only a handful of mega-cap tech stocks.

10. Sector Rotation

Watch whether money is flowing into:

Risk-on sectors:

Technology
Consumer Discretionary
Industrials
Communication Services

Or defensive sectors:

Utilities
Consumer Staples
Healthcare

Leadership by defensive sectors often suggests investors are becoming more cautious.

11. Insider Activity

Company executives buying their own stock is generally a positive sign.

Heavy insider selling alone isn't necessarily bearish, but broad insider buying is usually more informative.

12. Institutional Flows

Watch ETF flows into:

SPY
VOO
IVV

Strong inflows generally support prices.

If you want to build a comprehensive market dashboard

Given your interest in using macro indicators to guide investment decisions, I'd also add a few measures beyond the S&P 500 itself:

Macro: Fed policy, inflation, unemployment, PMI, GDP growth.
Sentiment: Fear & Greed Index, AAII sentiment, put/call ratio.
Global: U.S. Dollar Index, oil prices, gold prices, and the 10-year Treasury yield.
Market internals: Percentage of stocks above their 50- and 200-day moving averages, new highs vs. new lows, and equal-weight vs. cap-weight performance.

That combination provides a robust picture of trend, participation, valuation, liquidity, and macro conditions without becoming overwhelming.
